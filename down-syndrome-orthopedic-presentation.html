Orthopaedic Aspects of Down Syndrome

A Guide for Parents

Dr. Easwar T. Ramani
Senior Consultant Paediatric Orthopaedics & Scoliosis
Baby Memorial Hospital, Kozhikode & Kannur

Introduction

  • Children with Down syndrome have special orthopaedic needs
  • Early detection leads to better outcomes
  • Parents play a crucial role in identifying early signs
  • Regular screening is essential

Today's session will help you understand what to look for and when to seek medical attention.

Understanding the Basics

Key Factors

  • Hypotonia (low muscle tone)
  • Ligamentous laxity (loose ligaments)
  • Delayed motor development
  • Altered joint mechanics

Hypotonia & Ligamentous Laxity

What You Might Notice:

  • Delayed sitting, standing, or walking
  • Unusual flexibility in joints
  • "Floppy" appearance when held
  • Difficulty maintaining posture
  • Unusual walking patterns

How It Affects Your Child:

  • More prone to joint dislocations
  • May develop unusual posture
  • May tire more easily during activities
  • Can lead to foot, knee, or spine issues

Regular physical therapy helps strengthen muscles and improve stability!

Neck and Spine Issues

Atlantoaxial Instability (AAI)

  • Affects 10-20% of children with Down syndrome
  • Involves the top two vertebrae in the neck
  • Most concerning orthopaedic issue
  • Can potentially lead to spinal cord compression

Warning Signs for AAI

When to Contact Your Doctor Immediately:

  • Neck pain or discomfort
  • Abnormal head posture or tilting
  • Reduced neck movements
  • Changes in walking ability or frequent falls
  • Weakness in arms or legs
  • Changes in bowel or bladder control

These symptoms require prompt medical attention!

Other Spine Issues

Scoliosis

  • Sideways curvature of the spine
  • Affects 8-18% of individuals with Down syndrome
  • Often develops during growth spurts
  • Monitor for uneven shoulders or waistline

Kyphosis

  • Forward rounding of the upper back
  • May appear as "slouching"
  • Can affect breathing if severe
  • Often related to low muscle tone

Hip Problems

Hip Dysplasia & Dislocation

  • Abnormal hip socket development
  • Affects 1-7% of children with Down syndrome
  • Can progress over time if untreated
  • May cause limping or pain

Signs to Watch For:

  • Delayed walking
  • Limping or waddling gait
  • One leg appearing shorter
  • Pain in the hip, groin, or knee
  • Reduced hip movement

Hip Problems: Timeline

Under age 2: Delayed walking, unstable hips due to hypotonia and ligamentous laxity

Ages 2-8: Hip instability may progress to dislocations that can still be treated

After age 8: Hip problems become more difficult to correct

Adolescence/adulthood: Fixed dislocations may occur if untreated

Early detection before age 8 greatly improves treatment success!

Knee Problems

Common Issues:

  • Patellofemoral instability (kneecap problems)
  • Knock knees (genu valgum)
  • Ligament injuries

Signs to Watch For:

  • Knee pain during activities
  • Knees "giving way"
  • Difficulty with stairs
  • Unusual knee alignment
  • Swelling around the knee

Foot Problems

Common Issues:

  • Flat feet (affects ~50%)
  • In-toeing (feet point inward)
  • Clubfoot (in 1-7% of cases)
  • Bunions (hallux valgus)

Impact on Daily Life:

  • May affect walking ability
  • Can cause pain with activities
  • May require special shoes or inserts
  • Can lead to early fatigue

Many foot issues can be successfully managed with proper footwear, orthotics, or physical therapy!

Inflammatory Arthritis

An Often Missed Diagnosis

  • Down Syndrome-associated Arthritis (DA) is 6 times more common than in the general population
  • Often goes undiagnosed for an average of 2 years
  • Can affect multiple joints
  • Early treatment is crucial to prevent joint damage

Watch for: Joint swelling, morning stiffness, decreased activity, reluctance to use an arm or leg, or unexplained irritability

Bone Strength Concerns

  • Children with Down syndrome have lower bone density
  • Increases risk of fractures
  • Contributing factors:
    • Reduced physical activity
    • Nutritional factors
    • Hormonal differences
    • Genetic factors

Regular weight-bearing exercise, adequate calcium and vitamin D, and a balanced diet can help improve bone health!

Recommended Screening Timeline

Age What to Screen How Often
Birth to 6 months Hip instability At birth, 6 weeks, 6 months
Throughout childhood Developmental milestones At regular check-ups
Ages 3-5 Neck (AAI) and spine If symptoms present
School age through adolescence Spine (scoliosis) Yearly
Throughout childhood Feet and lower limbs At regular check-ups

When to Seek Medical Help

  • New pain that lasts more than a few days
  • Changes in walking ability or pattern
  • Refusal to use an arm or leg
  • Changes in posture or spinal alignment
  • Decreased activity level or energy
  • Joint swelling, redness, or warmth
  • Regression in motor skills or abilities
  • Any of the neck warning signs mentioned earlier

Trust your instincts! You know your child best.

Benefits of Early Intervention

Physical Therapy

  • Improves muscle strength
  • Enhances coordination
  • Promotes proper development
  • Teaches proper movement patterns

Early Medical Intervention

  • Prevents deformity progression
  • Reduces need for surgery
  • Improves long-term outcomes
  • Maximizes functional abilities

Early intervention can significantly improve your child's quality of life!

Common Treatment Approaches

  • Physical therapy: Strengthening, coordination, and mobility exercises
  • Orthotics: Supportive inserts or braces for feet, ankles, or spine
  • Mobility aids: When needed to enhance independence
  • Medications: For inflammatory conditions
  • Surgery: Reserved for specific situations when necessary

Treatment plans are always individualized to your child's specific needs.

Building Your Support Team

  • Pediatrician: Coordinates care and regular screenings
  • Pediatric Orthopedist: Specializes in musculoskeletal issues
  • Physical Therapist: Focuses on movement and strength
  • Occupational Therapist: Helps with daily living skills
  • Support Groups: Connect with other families

Communication between team members is key to comprehensive care!

Supporting Your Child Daily

At Home

  • Encourage active play
  • Provide proper supportive footwear
  • Continue therapy exercises at home
  • Ensure good posture during activities

At School

  • Share information with teachers
  • Consider ergonomic seating
  • Accommodate for physical education
  • Allow extra time for transitions

Success Stories

With proper screening, early intervention, and ongoing care, children with Down syndrome can:

  • Participate in sports and physical activities
  • Develop good mobility and independence
  • Experience less pain and fewer complications
  • Maintain better long-term joint health
  • Enjoy improved quality of life
